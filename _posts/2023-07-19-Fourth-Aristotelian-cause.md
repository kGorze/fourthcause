 ## Description of Aristotelian causes

Aristotle, in his _Physics_[^20] argued that there are four causes behind everything that exists. These causes answer the question of "How" or "Why" something is the way it is.

### In kind[^21]
The cause is called, first, 
- (1) matter[^22] from which something is made, for example, bronze is the cause of a statue and silver is the cause of a cup, and the types of bronze and silver. 

---

- (2) In another sense, the cause is form and pattern, i.e., the definition of essence and its types: for example, for an octave, the ratio of 2 : 1 and, in general, the number and parts covered by the definition. 

---

- (3) The cause is also the first principle of change or rest; for example, the counselor is the cause of the action, and the father is the cause of the child, and, in general, the perpetrator is the cause of the act, and that which causes change is the cause of that which is subject to change. 

---

- (4) The cause is also the purpose, that is, what something is for; for example, health is the cause of a walk. "Why does someone walk?" - we ask - "for health." And by answering yes we think we have given a cause. The same applies to all means occurring before the goal, when something else triggered the process of movement; for example, losing weight, laxation, medication, instruments are causes of health, because all these means were used for the sake of the goal, although they differ in that some are instruments and others are actions.

---


Thus, in so many meanings the word "cause" is used. From this variety of meanings of cause, it follows that for the same thing there are several causes, and not incidental: for example, the causes of a statue are sculptural art and bronze, not because of something else, but _as_ a statue; and not in the same way, but one as a material cause and the other as a cause of motion. The causes can also be reciprocal, for example, exercise is the cause of good health and health is the cause of exercise, but not in the same way, but one as the goal and the other as the source of movement. And again, the same cause can cause opposites; for when something is present, it causes a certain event, and when it is absent, we blame it for causing the opposite, for example, we attribute the sinking of a ship to the absence of the helmsman, while his presence is the cause of safety; both causes, presence and absence, are therefore causes of movement.

All the listed causes are clearly arranged into four groups. Letters are the cause of motions, matter is the cause of things produced, fire, earth and other such elements are the cause of bodies, parts are the cause of wholes, and premises are the cause of conclusions, insofar as they are what something is made of. Some are causes as _substrate_ (for example, parts), others as _essence_ (the whole, the arrangement of elements and form). Others are causes as the purpose and good of other things. The purposive cause is in the highest degree the good and end of all other things. We see no difference in whether one calls it a good or only an apparent good.

So many are the causes and such is the number of their types, but the varieties of causes are many, although when one generalizes, they are also relatively few. Causes can be divided according to different ratios; for example, among causes of the same kind, some are earlier, others later: for example, a doctor is earlier than health, and an artist is earlier than his work, a number and a double than an octave, and the general is always earlier than the particular things it includes.

There are still incidental causes and their types, for example, Polyclet is in one sense the cause of the statue, and in another sense the sculptor is the cause, because only incidentally the sculptor is Polyclet. There are also groups of incidental causes: for example, a man or, more generally, an animal is the cause of a statue, because Polyclet is a man and a man is an animal. Among these incidental causes, some are further away, others are closer; for example, if one were to say that the cause of the statue is white and educated, not just Polyclet or man. But in addition to all these varieties of causes, both proper and incidental, some are called capable causes; for example, the cause of a house under construction is the builder or the builder. Similarly, one can say of things whose causes are proper causes, for example, some thing can be called the cause of this particular statue, or statues, or paintings in general; of this particular bronze, or bronze, or matter in general; and similarly for incidental causes. Moreover, both incidental and proper causes can occur in combination; for example, one can say not "Polyclet" or "sculptor" but "Polyclet the sculptor."

While there are six varieties of causes, each can be expressed in two ways: for they are causes as an individual or as a kind, as an accidence or as a kind of accidence, as combined or singular, and all can exist either currently or potentially. But they differ in that operative, i.e. individual, causes exist simultaneously with the things of which they are causes, for example, this here is a man healing with a man recovering, and this here is a builder with this house being built; but this does not occur with potential causes, for the house does not die simultaneously with the builder.

### The fourth cause in the modern world
Now our post-materialist view of the Final Cause(Fourth one) is sort of different from Aristotle's original view. We have to remember that Aristotle viewed grammar and cognition as something that in some way was directly reflective of reality itself. Compare this view shared with the so-called "Speculative Grammarians" of the Middle Ages, "speculative" coming from the Latin word _speculum_ "mirror", since grammar reflects reality.

Aristotle originally had argued that it is appropriate to refer to the Final Cause of something whenever it is not due to randomness or spontaneity. The example he uses is the growth of human teeth: there is no variance in where the molar and incisors grow within the human mouth. Everything appears where it's "supposed to" and we can assume that there is some kind of Final Cause behind this.

If different shapes and sizes teeth grew in different locations of the mouth, then it would be appropriate to talk of them as lacking a Final Cause. Things that appear randomly and inconsistently do not necessarily have Final Causes, but if something happens invariably, we can trust that it has a Final Cause.

### Contemporary modernist thinking/Leaving the 3rd and 4th causes.
If you want to point your finger at a single philosophical change that defines the shift from the Aristotelean worldview of antiquity and the Middle Ages to the materialism of modernity, it is the rejection of the Formal and Final Causes in the early Enlightenment.

Does it make sense in today's world to want something other than simple survival. Recently, I've heard many opinions on the desirability of existence, and everyone has their own prescription. 

Just ask your modern brain: "Does everything _really_ have a purpose?" You will probably reflexively think back "No," therefore, you do not believe in a Final Cause to everything. The same is true of the Formal Cause, both of them seeming to assume that there is a kind of conscious agency behind the action. That isn't strictly speaking how Aristotle intended them, but that's how they are interpreted through modern goggles.

You can see their rejection as early as the 1600's: Francis Bacon[^1] in _Novum Organum_[^2] pushed aside the Final Cause as only being only suitable for inter-human behavior. The Formal Cause, he dismissed merely as _desperata_[^3] "hopeless." 

He actually dismissed the vocabulary of the other two causes as being superficial and an irrelevant distinction too, but philosophically, they are still retained in his philosophy by other terms.

In any case, modern people do not believe in Final and Formal Causes, or if they do, not for everything in the cosmos. 
For Aquinas and others in the Aristotelean world, the question of whether the universe has a purpose or a formal plan is a kind of tautology. Of course it does! Everything non-random does in Aristotelianism.

Nowadays we atomize questions like "Why" to the point that even causality itself doesn't mean anything and is a mere human cognitive convention, but for Aristotle, the linguistic existence of "Why" questions means that there is a legitimate logical equivalent to "Why" in reality.

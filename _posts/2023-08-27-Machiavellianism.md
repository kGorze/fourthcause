## Scholastic method: two main parts ^1

In the scholastic method of teaching, the central point was the work, which was commented on by the lecturer. The lecture (lectio) was mainly divided into two parts: **_divisio_ and _expositio_.



The master quoted a passage from the work and then, within a section called _divisio_, explained and arranged its contents in a logical order. The _Expositio_, or lecture of the text, was an essential part of the presentation. The lecturer aimed to uncover the deepest essence of the text and show its profound meaning.

I draw attention to two important points. In this phase of the process, the learner was not active, because he did not have the proper basis for engagement. He simply lacked knowledge of the subject, and therefore his main task was primarily to listen and acquire information. This is different from today's didactics, where the emphasis is on "activating the empty head," i.e. encouraging the student to be active, even if he lacks fundamental information on the topic and it is not clear for what purpose he is to be active.

The second important point is the parallels between the scholastic method of teaching and the ancient method, in which it was also paramount to explain the work and its content precisely, and then proceed to analy

## Scholasticism - types of discussion[^1]

It was only at this stage that the student (scholar) became active, since he already had knowledge that he could confront with the knowledge he had acquired earlier. The usual types of discussions were those between students and masters _disputationes ordinariae_ and the more solemn ones conducted between professors disputationes _quodlibetales_. There were also other types of discussions.

In these disputationes-exercises, first a problem was gradually developed. Later, accusations were made, arguments, counter-arguments, sophisms were put forward. Each problem was discussed in detail, which exercised precision and sharpness of thought. Paradoxically, this precision serves today as an indictment of the scholastic method itself. To make an accusation for having exercised logical thinking is simply unfair. Even when someone doesn't like the worldview of the Middle Ages, he or she should admire the pursuit of precision by the people of that time and take an example from them.

In this way of seeking knowledge, we can see the pattern of the classical method of teaching: first education, only then exercises to consolidate knowledge. The rejection of this scheme - on the religious level - suggests a belief in some variant of reincarnation (since a child is supposed to express himself on a subject before reading a book or listening to a lecture, he must have this knowledge from somewhere), and on the philosophical level it is a variant of extreme naturalism (belief in innate self-knowledge).

## Blasphemy

In "Reflections" or "The Prince" it is extremely important to understand the context. In Machiavelli's time, it was necessary to skillfully convey the content to the audience, as his views were read by different people. Any ruler who found his content unfavorable and blasphemous could impose the death penalty on him. For this reason, we can refer to his errors or omissions as covert forms of blasphemy.

Nowadays, we can also observe a similar phenomenon, now called whistle-blowing, which is generally viewed negatively. Back then, however, it could just as well be argued that covert blasphemy is more complicated than that overtly expressed.

There is some justification for this: in the case of typical blasphemy, the listener or reader is aware of its existence and does not personally participate in it. However, Machiavelli, by concealing his blasphemy, forces the reader to formulate the blasphemous thinking himself, making him in a way an accomplice of these contents."



## Unbelief. 

In his work "The Prince," Machiavelli recommends that rulers should be able to maintain a semblance of religiosity if it is beneficial to maintaining power. According to Machiavelli, a ruler should be able to use religion as a tool for social and political control. This could include displaying religiosity through public gestures such as attending masses or supporting religious institutions. Indeed, Machiavelli advised rulers to avoid open apostasy or outright warfare against the Church, as this could lead to internal destabilization and popular resistance.

However, Machiavelli was first and foremost a realist, not a religious philosopher. In his works, he stressed that rulers should act pragmatically and adapt their actions to specific circumstances in order to maintain their power. His views on religion were part of a general approach to politics, where morality was treated as a tool to achieve political goals, not as a determinant of behavior.

In summary, Machiavelli did not reject religion, but treated it as an element that rulers should skillfully use to consolidate their power and ensure stability. His approach to religion was pragmatic and subordinated to the desire to strengthen political power.

Nowadays, the most well-known varieties of the unbelief approach are polite indifference (liberalism) and nostalgia for a lost faith, combined with the inability to distinguish theological truth from myth (features of conservatism)."
